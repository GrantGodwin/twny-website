# Website Positioning Notes

**Project:** TWNY public website refresh · **Date:** 2026-06-17
**Purpose:** Turn the external research + the Atlas commercial model into positioning guidance for the public site. Covers research topics 12–19 and closes with the concise **Recommended Direction**.

> Legend: **[FACT]** observed / from the Atlas source model · **[INTERPRETATION]** · **[RECOMMENDATION]**.
> Assumptions about the client are labelled **[ASSUMPTION]** and should be validated, not treated as proven.

## 12. Presenting M365, AI, websites, hosting, domains, backup, Copilot & consulting as ONE coherent offer

**[FACT]** The current public site presents these as four disconnected icon cards. The Atlas model already supplies the unifying idea: **one managed tenancy underneath everything** ("one tenant, one source of truth — twny owns or governs the M365 tenant, the DNS, the backup, and the web platform"), and **one operating loop** every engagement runs (Discover → Recommend → Quote → Deliver → Operate → Review).

**[RECOMMENDATION]** Frame the offer as a single operation with one spine, not a service menu:
- **The spine sentence:** *"We run the cloud and the website so you can run the business"* (already a `public` line in the model). Everything hangs off "we run it."
- **Group, don't list.** Two clusters: **(1) Run your business on Microsoft 365** — productivity tiers, identity/security, backup, Copilot; **(2) Run your web presence** — hosting/care, domains. **Consulting** is the named "safety valve" beside both ("projects, quoted separately so we can scope them properly").
- **Make the tenancy the through-line:** "It all sits on one tenant we own and look after — your email, files, identity, backup, domain and (where applicable) website, with one source of truth and clean ownership you can always take with you."
- **Use the loop to show *how*, the clusters to show *what*, the boundaries to show *where it stops*.** Coherence comes from those three being the same on every page.

## 13. Ideal small-business client — assumptions

**[FACT — from the Atlas client-fit model]** Ideal = small business / professional services / sole traders & founders with growth intent; already on or willing to be on Microsoft 365; values clean operations; comfortable with "managed service" framing; "has a budget and wants the problem to go away." Acceptable = some legacy but willing to modernise. Explicitly **not** for: residential/break-fix, "unlimited support" expectations, price-shoppers comparing to raw CSP licence cost.

**[ASSUMPTION]** The buyer is typically the **owner/operator or office manager**, not an IT department; non-technical but competent; time-poor; has been burned by either DIY sprawl or a generic MSP; in Australia, billing in AUD ex-GST; team size roughly 1–25 seats.

**[RECOMMENDATION]** Write to that one person. Speak to outcomes ("someone who already knows your tenant when it breaks"), not specs. Let the site gently **disqualify** the wrong fit (residential, break-fix, "unlimited") — this protects margin and reads as confidence.

## 14. Buying triggers

**[INTERPRETATION / from the model's qualifying questions]** Likely moments that bring this buyer to the site:
- Starting a business / hiring first staff and needing email on their own domain done properly.
- Inheriting a mess (ex-IT person left; tenant set up badly; mixed personal/business accounts).
- A scare — phishing, a deleted folder, a near-miss — driving the "what if?" backup conversation.
- A domain about to lapse, or DNS/email-deliverability problems.
- Outgrowing ad-hoc IT / a generalist MSP that absorbs everything and explains nothing.
- Wanting to "use AI / Copilot" but not knowing where it fits or whether their data is ready.
- A website that's neglected, insecure, or that nobody is actually looking after.

**[RECOMMENDATION]** Surface these as entry points (the service pages and the recommendation flow should each answer one trigger directly). The guided **`/recommend`** flow (8 plain questions → one recommended starting point → "talk to us") is the ideal trigger-to-conversation bridge and should be a primary path.

## 15. Objections & trust barriers

**[INTERPRETATION]** Given the 3–4× price step from the current/commodity market:
- **"Why is this more than [reseller]?"** → because the licence is the cost, the *managed wrapper* is the product; counter with explicit inclusions + "we run it" outcomes.
- **"What exactly do I get / where does it stop?"** → explicit inclusions/exclusions and the consulting boundary, stated up front.
- **"Will I be locked in?"** → portability guarantee (domain, mailbox data, backup exports, website content; customer is always the registrant of record).
- **"Is this a real company or one person?"** → a named operator with a credible Modern Workplace background, plain founder voice, honest SLAs.
- **"Annual commitment feels risky."** → frame positively: monthly flex available; annual = two months free; no hidden premium.
- **"Can I trust the AI pitch?"** → the category is full of vapourware; TWNY counters with caution and readiness-first framing (Copilot needs a clean tenant first).

**[RECOMMENDATION]** Pre-empt every one of these on the page (a pricing FAQ + a boundaries section), in the Atlas voice. Do **not** invent testimonials or certifications to manufacture trust; build it from clarity, specificity, named accountability, and transparent pricing.

## 16. Homepage implications

**[RECOMMENDATION]** Structure (per the model's "Position, prove, point"):
1. **Hero:** wordmark + "Modern cloud operations and digital presence for small business." / "We run the cloud and the website so you can run the business." Single **Ember** CTA → `/recommend`.
2. **The loop:** Discover → Recommend → Quote → Deliver → Operate → Review as a Mineral structural strip (how we work).
3. **What you actually get:** the "surface vs underlying need" pairs (e.g. "A managed mailbox → someone to call when it breaks, who already knows your tenant").
4. **The offer, in two clusters** (M365/operations + web/presence) on one managed tenancy; consulting beside them.
5. **Why we're different:** 3–4 principles reworded — boundaries protect quality, modern by default, portability always, premium is clarity.
6. **Pricing posture:** annual vs monthly flex, "all prices published, ex-GST" → `/pricing`.
7. **Point:** "Tell us what you need; we'll recommend a starting point." Honest, low-pressure.
No stock hero, no slogans, no badge wall.

## 17. Services-page implications

**[RECOMMENDATION]** One consistent template per service (the Atlas data already supplies every part — public fields only):
title + plain positioning → plain-English explainer → tier ladder (with Annual/Monthly toggle) → public comparison matrix → what's included / not included → boundaries ("this is consulting") → bundling/eligibility callouts → trust reassurance (portability for domains; "recovery is the product" for backup) → CTA. **Add a Copilot/AI page** the current site lacks, written with caution (readiness-first, enablement scoped separately). Reframe **hosting** entirely from specs to *care* (continuity → active care → improvement), and **backup** from "20GB storage" to *managed recovery*.

## 18. Pricing-page implications

**[FACT]** Atlas decision D10: "publish all pricing, prominently." Two modes only: Annual Commitment (10× monthly = two months free) and Monthly Flex. All ex-GST, AUD.
**[RECOMMENDATION]** A real `/pricing` page with: a global **Annual / Monthly Flex toggle**; per-domain tier ladders using the public comparison rows; onboarding shown as a clear one-off with waiver stated plainly; add-ons (Backup, Copilot, Domains) and Consulting formats; one global GST line; **no margins, cost bases, or internal rationale**. Borrow Source Digital's transparency mechanics (ranges, "no scope creep," pricing FAQ) but in TWNY's restrained visual language, not dark-SaaS cards. Confirm prices are signed off (Atlas data is still `draft`) before publishing.

## 19. Trust / credibility implications

**[RECOMMENDATION]** Build credibility from substance, not theatre:
- **A named operator** + genuine Modern Workplace background (available publicly; confirm willingness to foreground it).
- **Explicit scope** (what we do / don't do) — turning the wrong customer away is a trust signal.
- **Portability & ownership hygiene** stated plainly.
- **Honest SLAs** (business hours, explicit best-effort out of hours) — honesty as differentiator.
- **Transparent pricing** itself as the primary trust artifact.
- **Quiet, substantiated partner capability** (Microsoft, Acronis) — capability, not a logo wall.
- **Do not fabricate** testimonials, client names, certifications, or experience figures (and fix the current "30 years" vs "launched 2016" contradiction by simply not making unverifiable claims).

## Recommended Direction (concise)

**What the TWNY public website should become:**

A **calm, premium, operator-credible site that sells a managed operation, not a price list.** It should make one promise — *"we run your cloud and your website so you can run the business"* — and prove it three ways the whole market currently fails to combine:

1. **Coherence.** One managed tenancy and one operating loop unify Microsoft 365, backup, domains, Copilot, hosting and consulting into a single operation — not four disconnected service cards.
2. **Clarity as premium.** Restrained, document-grade visual language (wordmark-only, Ink/Steel/Mineral/Ember, no gradients/stock/clipart), plain "decision + evidence + action" copy, and **transparent two-mode pricing** with explicit boundaries. In a field that is either cheap-and-generic, warm-but-vague, or transparent-but-template, TWNY occupies the open intersection: **premium, specific, transparent, and human.**
3. **Honest edges.** Named accountability, stated scope (including what TWNY won't do), portability guarantees, and honest SLAs — credibility built from substance, with nothing invented.

It is a **repositioning, not a reskin**: away from the current reseller/commodity site (near-cost pricing, spec-sold hosting, badge-and-clipart template, no AI, no point of view) toward the managed practice the Atlas brand and commercial model already define. The deliberate consequence is that it will turn away price-shoppers and speak precisely to the small-business operator who wants the problem to disappear reliably — and is willing to pay for clarity.
