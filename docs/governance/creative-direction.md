# TWNY Website — Creative Direction

**Status:** Governing document. Primary source of truth for the TWNY public website.
**Audience:** Anyone — human or AI — designing, writing, or building this site.

This is not a strategy document. The strategy lives in [`../research/`](../research/).
This is the standard we hold every design and content decision to, and the reasoning
behind it, so that a hundred small choices made by different people on different days
still add up to one coherent, premium website.

Read it the way you'd take direction from a creative director. The *why* matters as
much as the *what*, because the why is what lets you make the right call in a
situation this document didn't predict.

## North Star

> **The TWNY website should feel like the digital presence of a business people
> aspire to work with — not a business trying to convince people to buy from it.**

This is the lens for every decision. Aspiration attracts the buyer we want;
persuasion repels them. A site that *sells* signals it needs to; a site that simply
*is* — composed, confident, quietly excellent — makes people lean in. We are building
the second kind.

Two ideas follow from it and govern everything below:

- **The website exists to help the right small-business owner feel confident enough
  to start a conversation.** Everything else is secondary.
- **The website is an emotional and commercial experience, not an operational system.**
  It expresses TWNY's personality, confidence, and taste — it does not document its
  machinery.

If a choice serves the North Star, it belongs. If it's there to convince, impress, or
explain for its own sake, cut it.

## 1. Purpose of the website

The website does one job: **turn the right small-business owner from a stranger into a
conversation** — by making them *feel*, within seconds, that we are calmer, clearer,
and more capable than anyone else they're considering, and by quietly letting the
wrong buyer leave on their own.

It is not a brochure, a catalogue, or a lead-vacuum. It sells a managed operation at a
premium, and that price is only credible if the experience itself feels premium — the
way a beautifully made object justifies its cost before you've read a word about it.

So we design for **feeling first, then comprehension**. A visitor should sense quality
before they grasp the offer, trust the operator before they read the pricing, and want
the conversation before they've finished scrolling. Atmosphere earns the attention;
clarity earns the decision. Both matter, in that order.

Success looks like: the right visitor books a call feeling calm and confident; the
wrong visitor (price-shopper, break-fix, "unlimited support") leaves without
contacting us. Both are wins.

## 2. Atlas and the website

Atlas is TWNY's internal operating system — the engine room. It is rigorous,
structural, and built for operators. The public website is the showroom — built for a
time-poor business owner who has never heard the word "tenancy" and never will care.

> **Atlas informs TWNY's commercial thinking. The public website expresses TWNY's
> personality, confidence, and experience.**

The two must not be confused, because their audiences and their jobs are opposite.

**Atlas legitimately informs:**

- the commercial model (managed operation, one tenancy, "we run it"),
- the service model and what's included where,
- pricing structure and the two recurring modes,
- terminology and the operational loop,
- the disciplined, honest way of thinking about scope and boundaries.

**Atlas must not dictate:**

- page layouts or homepage composition,
- visual hierarchy or art direction,
- emotional tone,
- typography, imagery, depth, or motion.

The standing temptation is to let the engine room design the showroom — to reach for
the structural, table-and-rule language of an internal tool because it feels precise
and safe. Resist it. Precision in an operational system reads as competence; the same
precision on a public site reads as cold. When a choice is about *what we offer or
what's true*, defer to Atlas. When it's about *how it looks and feels*, defer to this
document.

## 3. Target audience

Design every page for **one person**: the owner/operator or office manager of an
Australian small business, roughly 1–25 seats. Non-technical but competent. Time-poor.
Decision-maker and budget-holder in one chair. They don't want to learn about
technology; they want it handled, and they want to trust whoever handles it.

This dictates altitude and tone. One person making a confident decision needs
*reassurance, atmosphere, and legibility* — not feature depth or vendor jargon. They
won't read a spec sheet; they'll feel the quality of the room and read three honest
sentences. If a page would satisfy a procurement committee but feel cold to a sole
trader, it's wrong.

Secondary readers — the founder setting up properly from day one, and the trusted
advisor (bookkeeper, designer) who refers them in — are served, but never at the
primary reader's expense.

## 4. Non-target audience

Who we are **not** for, and the site should quietly make that clear:

- Price-shoppers comparing only to raw Microsoft licence cost.
- People expecting "unlimited support" for anything technology-shaped.
- Residential, break-fix, home-IT, printers, Wi-Fi, hardware repair.
- Large legacy estates with no modernisation budget; on-prem server shops.
- Anyone who argues every exclusion.

Turning these visitors away is **a feature, not a leak**. A site that tries to be for
everyone signals it has no point of view — and premium brands are confident about who
they're for. Never water down a boundary to keep a wrong-fit lead.

## 5. What TWNY is really selling

We are not selling mailboxes, hosting, gigabytes, or licences. **We are selling the
problem going away, reliably, under one accountable operator — and the calm that comes
with it.**

Always lead with the need, not the surface:

- A managed mailbox → *someone to call when it breaks who already knows your setup.*
- A website → *a presence that reflects the business and stays up.*
- Backup → *insurance for the day you delete the wrong folder or get phished.*
- Domains → *certainty the name you've owned for years won't lapse or get hijacked.*

The unifying product is **one managed operation on one tenancy** — "we run it." The
market sells four disconnected service cards; our advantage *is* the coherence. Never
present the offer as a menu of unrelated things.

## 6. Brand personality

TWNY is **a capable operator with taste** — competent and human. Picture the most
trusted person you know explaining exactly what they'll do, what it costs, and where
it stops, in a beautifully made room, in natural light, without raising their voice.

The register is **calm, premium, editorial, modern, trustworthy, human, practical,
quietly confident** — closer to an architecture practice, a boutique interior studio,
or an industrial-design house than to any IT company. Confidence expressed through
restraint and quality, never volume.

Premium is not the absence of feeling; it is feeling, controlled. So *cold and
clinical* is as wrong as *loud and salesy*. The personality is the differentiator
because the entire market sounds and looks the same — and the fastest way to be
forgettable is to join it. Ours lives in taste, restraint, and what we refuse to do.

## 7. Emotional goals

A visitor should move through three feelings, in order:

1. **Relief** — "Oh good, someone competent will just handle this."
2. **Confidence** — "I understand exactly what I'm getting and what it costs."
3. **Trust** — "These people are honest; they told me where it stops and that I can leave any time."

Judge every decision against this arc. Anything that creates anxiety (hidden pricing,
vague scope, pressure), confusion (jargon, clutter, too many choices), or doubt
(unproven claims, fake urgency) works against it, however nice it looks. We earn the
call by lowering the visitor's blood pressure and making them feel they've arrived
somewhere considered — not by raising their FOMO.

## 8. Copywriting principles

Words and art direction are equal partners: photography sets the mood, words make the
promise. Hold the line on words:

- **Plain English, one idea per sentence.** The customer is the subject: "You own your domain," not "Domain ownership is provided."
- **Decision + evidence + action.** Say the thing, back it, point somewhere.
- **Specific true things beat claims.** "We run the Microsoft 365 tenant, the DNS, the backup and the website — one source of truth" beats "Made Easy." Specificity *is* credibility.
- **Headlines can be confident and human.** Write a real sentence worth setting large, not a tagline. Editorial presence comes from a strong idea in big type, not from a slogan.
- **Gloss every technical term immediately** — "the tenant, the account that holds your whole organisation." Never assume knowledge; never condescend.
- **Boundaries are content.** Saying what we don't do builds more trust than another benefit would.
- **"We", never "TWNY", in body copy.** The brand name lives in the masthead.
- **Let copy breathe.** Short paragraphs, generous space; white space is part of the writing.
- **Banned words:** *solutions, leverage, empower, synergy, transformation, disruption, ecosystem, fit for purpose, take the hassle out, unlock, seamless, cutting-edge, world-class.* If a sentence needs one, the thought underneath isn't finished.
- **Never invent proof.** No fabricated testimonials, client names, certifications, or experience figures. Honesty is the brand.

## 9. The visual system

The palette and type are the brand's foundation — a foundation to build on, not a cage.
They give the site a consistent voice; art direction, photography, and depth give it
presence.

- **Colour.** *Ink* anchors text; *Steel* structures; *Mineral* (`#1379A0`) carries movement — links, navigation, interactive accents; *Sand* and *Paper* are quiet ground tones; *Ember* (`#D05E2E`) is the **decision colour, and the only one** — every primary call to action is Ember, and Ember is nothing else. This one discipline does more for clarity than any other rule; keep it absolutely, because the moment everything is a CTA colour, nothing is. Beyond these anchors, photographic colour and tonal atmosphere are welcome — a site can be rich and warm without inventing new brand colours.
- **Typography is the primary instrument.** Hierarchy, scale, weight, and spacing do most of the work. Use a wide, confident scale: large, quietly dramatic display; comfortable, readable body; restrained fine labels. Type sets the tone before a word is read.
- **Light and dark are both first-class, and both can be cinematic.** Light is crisp, warm, editorial — never clinical. Dark is rich and atmospheric — never a flat slab.
- **The identity is the wordmark only.** Lowercase `twny`. No monogram, badge, circular device, or favicon mark — a single confident wordmark is more premium than any emblem.

What is genuinely off-limits is not any technique but *genericness*: equal-card
"dashboard" grids as a default layout, clip-art and icon collages, stock "people at
laptops," AI orbs and nodes, neon, and effects used as decoration. We avoid these
because they belong to everyone, and the whole point is to belong to no one else.

## 10. Art Direction

The website should feel art-directed — composed, intentional, editorial — the way a
good magazine, an architecture practice's site, or a luxury product page feels.
Premium visuals don't shout; they glide.

- **Immersive hero imagery.** Key pages open with a strong, full-bleed photograph or a richly composed image space — an authentic environment, natural light, a sense of place. The hero is a *beginning, not a summary*: it sets mood and direction, it does not explain the offer.
- **Full-height opening sections.** Let the first view own the screen: image, one strong headline, one supporting line, one Ember invitation. A confident opening communicates quality instantly; cramming three messages above the fold communicates anxiety.
- **Transparent navigation over imagery.** The header sits *over* the hero — transparent or lightly veiled — then resolves to a solid, legible state on scroll. It's a premium signal, and it must never cost legibility (hold contrast; add a subtle scrim where needed).
- **Oversized typography.** Scale is our cheapest, most powerful tool for presence. Pair large display type with restrained body copy so the size reads as confidence, not noise.
- **Editorial composition and rhythm.** Think in spreads, not boxes. Vary section shape and pacing — full-bleed image, then a calm text column, then a wide quiet band — so the page has cadence. A strong underlying grid is what lets variation feel composed rather than chaotic.
- **Asymmetry where it earns its place.** Centred everything is a template tell. Off-set headlines and asymmetric image/text splits create energy and sophistication — always anchored to the grid, so they read as intent, not accident.
- **Premium spacing.** Treat whitespace as a material. Generous, deliberate space signals confidence; crowding signals insecurity. When unsure, add space.
- **Layered depth.** Build atmosphere by layering image, soft overlay, type, and subtle foreground, so the page has a believable sense of space (see §12). Depth supports hierarchy; it is never the feature.
- **Restrained, purposeful animation.** Motion should feel like physics, not performance: gentle fades and rises as content enters, smooth state transitions, easing that feels expensive. Nothing bouncy, looping, or attention-seeking — and nothing the page depends on.
- **Premium interaction design.** Hovers, focus, and transitions should feel crafted and consistent — quiet confirmation, never spectacle. Quality is felt in the details.

The test for any art-direction decision: *does this make the visitor feel they've
arrived somewhere considered and trustworthy?* If yes, it earns its place. If it's
there to look clever, cut it.

## 11. Photography and imagery

Photography is a core part of how the site creates emotion, confidence, and context.
The right image does what no paragraph can: it makes a cautious owner feel *this is for
people like me.*

- **Photograph emotion and context, not product.** Show the world our clients live in — real Australian small businesses, workplaces and workshops, considered interiors, architecture, craftsmanship, hands at work, natural light, authentic environments. Evoke *calm competence and human warmth.*
- **Technology should rarely be shown.** Almost never screens, devices, server rooms, code, or "people pointing at monitors." We sell the calm *around* the technology, not the technology.
- **Editorial stills, not stock.** Images should look authored — intentional light, a clear focal point, honest moments. If an image could appear on a competitor's site, it's the wrong image.
- **Natural light and a timeless treatment.** Prefer soft, real light and a calm, slightly restrained grade over saturated, trend-driven looks. The photography's palette should feel of a piece with the brand's quiet tones.
- **Truth over polish.** No fabricated "clients," no staged offices passed off as ours. Where genuine, specific imagery doesn't exist, choose authentic environmental photography that is clearly atmospheric in spirit — never a staged claim.
- **Restraint still applies.** Fewer, stronger images beat many; one arresting hero beats a gallery. Well-composed empty space remains a premium choice. The goal is never *more* imagery — it is *intentional* imagery.

## 12. Depth, layering and visual effects

Effects are governed by a single rule of judgement:

> **A visual effect is welcome when it improves hierarchy, depth, or atmosphere. It is
> wrong when it becomes the feature, or when it's there to look modern.**

Applied:

- **Gradients** — welcome when subtle and atmospheric: a soft tonal wash, a radial glow behind a hero, a gentle darkening that lifts type off an image. Wrong as the loud two-colour hero gradient that screams "tech startup." Test: does it create depth and mood, or just decorate?
- **Transparency and glass** — welcome for layering and for navigation over imagery, where a translucent surface adds depth and keeps content legible. Wrong when applied everywhere as a stylistic gimmick.
- **Shadow and elevation** — welcome, used softly, to establish real layering and focus. Wrong when heavy, dark, or sprayed across every element to fake interest.
- **Overlays and scrims** — welcome, and often required, to hold text contrast over photography. This is craft, not decoration.
- **Depth generally** — aim for a believable sense of space through quiet layering of image, tone, and type, always in service of guiding the eye.

If you cannot name the hierarchy or atmosphere an effect serves, remove it. Effects are
seasoning; the meal is composition, type, and image.

## 13. Homepage principles

The homepage does one job — **position, prove, point** — as an *experience*, not a
document.

- **Open with presence.** An immersive, near-full-height hero: a strong environmental image, transparent navigation over it, one confident headline, one supporting line, one Ember invitation. Mood and trust first; the full offer can wait a few scrolls.
- **Prove with calm structure, beautifully composed.** Reveal what they get (the surface→need truths), then *how* we work (the operational loop as a refined, well-spaced sequence — editorial, not an icon strip), then the offer as one coherent operation. Editorial rhythm and depth keep proving from feeling like a manual.
- **Establish the wrapper before the price.** Let the value of the managed operation land *before* linking to pricing, so the number reads as "fair for all that," not "expensive."
- **Close as an invitation, not a funnel.** "Tell us what you need; we'll recommend a starting point." One Ember CTA, framed conversationally, surrounded by space. No urgency, no pop-ups, no countdowns.

The sequence is deliberate: reassurance first (or they bounce), differentiation second
(or they don't see why we cost more), transparency third (or they don't trust the
number). Reorder it and you lose someone at each step.

## 14. Service page principles

Service pages share a **consistent underlying spine** — consistency is itself a trust
signal — but they are art-directed pages, not forms. The spine: positioning → plain
explainer → what's included / where it stops → pricing → reassurance (ownership /
portability) → invitation.

- **Outcomes first, mechanics second.** Lead with the need; support with glossed detail.
- **Reframe commodities as care.** Hosting is *care and attention*, not RAM and storage. Backup is *managed recovery* — "recovery is the product, not storage" — not gigabytes.
- **State the edges.** What's included and, explicitly, what isn't. One-off work is consulting, quoted separately.
- **Reassure on ownership.** The customer can always leave with their data and is the registrant of record. Say it plainly.
- **Give each page its own atmosphere** within the shared spine — its own opening image and mood. The same hand, a different room.
- **Only publish what's public.** Never expose margins, cost bases, internal rationale, or client-fit labels.

## 15. Pricing principles

Pricing is our strongest trust artifact. **Publish it, prominently — and present it
beautifully.** Clarity and beauty are not in tension; clarity, well-composed, is itself
a luxury signal.

- **Transparency is the strategy.** Real numbers reinforce premium and filter wrong-fit leads before they email. Hidden "book a call for pricing" funnels are the opposite of who we are.
- **Two recurring modes only:** Annual Commitment (prepaid, two months free) and Monthly Flex (no lock-in). Same service either way; no penalty dressed as a discount.
- **All prices ex-GST, in AUD, stated once, never buried.**
- **Onboarding is one honest one-off,** its waiver rule stated plainly.
- **Refined, scannable presentation — not commodity cards, not a cold spreadsheet.** Borrow the mechanics of good transparent pricing (ranges, "no scope creep," a pricing FAQ that pre-empts objections) and render them with editorial calm: strong type, generous space, honest comparison.
- **Never show old reseller numbers, margins, or cost bases.**
- **Sign-off before publishing.** Until prices are confirmed, any figure is clearly flagged as draft; we do not quietly ship numbers we won't hold.

The pricing FAQ should answer the real objections out loud: *why more than a reseller,
annual vs monthly, why ex-GST, what's included, am I locked in.*

## 16. Balancing the forces

Six forces shape every page: **SEO, accessibility, storytelling, trust, commercial
clarity, and conversion.** A weak site lets one dominate — all-SEO reads like keyword
soup, all-conversion turns manipulative, all-storytelling forgets to sell, all-clarity
becomes a spec sheet.

**None dominates, and done well they reinforce each other:**

- Good **storytelling** *is* good **conversion** here — emotion and trust are what make this buyer act.
- Genuine **trust** *is* premium **brand**; honesty and clarity are the aesthetic.
- **Accessibility** and **SEO** are largely the same craft — semantic, well-structured, legible content serves humans, screen readers, and search at once.
- **Commercial clarity** keeps storytelling honest; **storytelling** keeps clarity warm.

Practical guidance for the two technical forces:

- **SEO:** be genuinely the clearest, most specific answer, and let structure be machine-legible as a by-product of being human-legible. Plain titles and meta descriptions a person would actually search; one clear `<h1>` per page; honest heading hierarchy; specific local language ("Australian small business," named services) over "any size, any industry." No keyword stuffing, doorway pages, or fake schema.
- **Accessibility:** target WCAG 2.1 AA in both light and dark. Semantic structure and landmarks; correct heading order; a skip link; full keyboard operability with a visible, well-styled focus state. Maintain text contrast over imagery (scrims exist for this). Honour reduced-motion — the site must be fully usable, and still feel premium, with motion off. Never rely on colour alone to carry meaning.

When forces seem to conflict, resolve toward **trust and the human** — they are what
make this particular sale.

## 17. Guidance for AI

This site will largely be built and maintained by AI assistants, whose strongest pull
is toward the generic average of the web — which is exactly what we reject.

**When making design decisions, optimise in this order:**

1. **Trust before explanation** — make them believe before you make them understand.
2. **Confidence before completeness** — a calm, clear page beats a thorough, cluttered one. Leave things out.
3. **Conversation before conversion-rate tricks** — invite a human exchange; never use fake urgency, manipulative pop-ups, dark patterns, or "limited time" pressure.
4. **Beauty before novelty** — choose the timeless, well-composed option over the clever or trendy one.
5. **Restraint before decoration** — when unsure, remove. Composition, type, and image carry the page; effects only season it.

**And in practice:**

- **Do not fall back to generic web templates.** No default SaaS hero gradient, equal-card feature grid, stock "people at laptops," AI orbs, or template agency layout. If a choice feels like the obvious web default, distrust it.
- **This Creative Direction outranks your instincts.** When training says "add the standard hero," this document decides.
- **Honour the system; extend it with taste.** Use the established palette (Ember-only CTA especially), type, and spine. Introduce photography, depth, and editorial composition within §10–§12, but don't invent new brand colours or fonts.
- **Never invent content,** even temporarily. Flag drafts and unverified figures visibly.
- **When unsure, stop and ask, or re-read the research** — don't guess and ship.
- **Touch only what the task needs.**

## 18. Future design philosophy

As the site grows, hold the same centre of gravity: **timeless, editorial, premium —
not trend-driven, and never busier than it needs to be.**

We are not chasing what looks current; we are aiming for what will still feel
considered in ten years — the longevity of good architecture and good print. Depth,
photography, and motion are welcome *because they serve atmosphere and hierarchy*;
trends are declined *because they date*. When a popular effect appears, ask whether it
will read as timeless or as "of its year," and choose timeless.

Every addition is measured against one question: *does this make the right client's
decision easier, and the experience more trustworthy?* — not "is this nice to have?"
New services join the shared spine with their own art direction; new sections earn
their place by deepening trust or clarity, not by adding interest. The day the site
feels like a generic marketing site — or a back-office tool — is the day it has lost
what makes it work.

## 19. Design Review Questions

Before anything is built, review the proposed design against these questions. They are
deliberately uncomfortable; their job is to catch the generic, the over-explained, and
the merely decorative *before* implementation. Answer honestly.

- **Would this still feel premium if every animation were removed?** If the quality lives in motion, the underlying composition isn't strong enough yet.
- **Does the first screen create an emotional response before asking anyone to understand information?** Feeling should arrive before comprehension.
- **Could this belong to any MSP, IT consultancy, or digital agency?** If yes, redesign it. Interchangeable is the one thing we cannot be.
- **Have we reduced trust by trying to explain too much?** Over-explaining reads as insecurity; confidence leaves things unsaid.
- **Is every visual element improving hierarchy or atmosphere, rather than decorating?** If an element can't name the job it does, remove it.
- **Would removing half the content make the page stronger?** Usually it would. Find what to cut.
- **Would someone outside the technology industry aspire to have a website like this?** If not, we're still designing an IT website, not a premium brand.
- **Does this feel like a business people aspire to work with, or one trying to convince them to buy?** Anything in the second register fails the North Star.
- **Is Ember doing one job — the single decision — and nothing else?** If it's scattered, the hierarchy has collapsed.
- **If a stranger saw only this page for five seconds, what would they feel?** If the honest answer isn't "calm, capable, premium," keep working.

A design that cannot survive these questions is not ready, however polished it looks.

## 20. Implementation checklist

Before shipping any change, confirm:

- [ ] I have read the relevant Creative Direction sections (not skimmed them).
- [ ] It passes the **Design Review Questions** above.
- [ ] It makes the right owner **feel** more confident, calm, and ready to talk — not just more informed.
- [ ] It improves **trust** through clarity, honesty, or accountability, and erodes none of it.
- [ ] It serves the **ideal client** (the small-business operator deciding alone), not a committee or an IT department.
- [ ] The **copy** is plain, specific, human, free of banned words, and invents no proof.
- [ ] It is **art-directed**, not defaulted: considered composition, type, space, and — where used — photography and depth.
- [ ] Any **imagery** creates emotion and context, looks authored not stock, and rarely shows technology.
- [ ] Any **effect** serves hierarchy or atmosphere and is not the feature.
- [ ] **Ember is the only CTA colour,** used sparingly.
- [ ] **Pricing** (if touched) is transparent, ex-GST, two-mode, beautifully clear, and signed off or flagged draft.
- [ ] It is **accessible**: semantic, keyboard-operable, AA-contrast over imagery in light and dark, and fully usable with motion off.
- [ ] It reflects **Atlas's substance** but not Atlas's surface.
- [ ] If anything here was a "no," I stopped and reconsidered rather than shipping anyway.
