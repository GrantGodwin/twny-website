# TWNY Website Art Direction

**Status:** Governing art-direction document for the TWNY public website refresh.  
**Created:** 2026-06-18.  
**Audience:** Designers, frontend developers, writers, and AI assistants working on the public website.

This document answers what the public website should become visually and emotionally. It does not provide HTML, wireframes, components, or production layouts. It defines the creative standard strongly enough that a competent frontend developer or designer can build the homepage without guessing.

Where this document conflicts with `creative-direction.md`, this document is the more specific visual authority for Homepage V3 and the public art direction.

## The Decision

TWNY should look like **the calm operating layer behind a successful modern small business**.

Not an internal operating system.  
Not an architecture magazine.  
Not an MSP template.  
Not an AI startup.  
Not a marketing agency.

The art direction is **Modern Operator Editorial**:

- **Modern**, because the work is Microsoft 365, AI, websites, domains, backup, automation, and the systems that let a small business run properly now.
- **Operator**, because TWNY quietly looks after the hidden technology, owns the details, and stays accountable after launch.
- **Editorial**, because the site should feel composed, premium, selective, and human, with strong pacing, visual hierarchy, and enough atmosphere to create confidence before the visitor reads every detail.

The homepage must communicate modern business first and technology second, but within the first few seconds it must still be unmistakably obvious that TWNY is a premium technology practice.

If a visitor admires the site but cannot tell what TWNY does, the art direction has failed.

## The Place Between The Extremes

Previous homepage directions failed in opposite ways.

**Atlas leaked into the public site.** The first direction treated the website like a public version of an internal system: structural, operational, diagram-led, and precise. That was useful for truth but wrong for persuasion. It made TWNY feel like software, not a practice. The public buyer is not trying to operate Atlas. They are trying to trust someone to handle technology.

**V2 became an architecture/interior magazine.** The second direction found atmosphere, restraint, photography, and premium quiet. Those are valuable discoveries. But the subject drifted too far from technology. The hero could belong to a cafe, architect, furniture brand, or interiors studio. TWNY disappeared behind the mood.

The correct answer sits between those poles:

- Keep Atlas's truth, but not Atlas's surface.
- Keep V2's calm, scale, and confidence, but not its lifestyle ambiguity.
- Make the site feel premium through composition, pacing, photography, type, and restraint.
- Make the site feel like technology through visible operational evidence, service language, modern work context, and precise hierarchy.

## Existing Direction: What Is Right And What Must Change

The existing `creative-direction.md` is right about several important things:

- TWNY should build confidence, not pressure.
- Premium comes from restraint, clarity, and taste.
- The offer is a managed operation, not a menu of disconnected services.
- Ember must remain the decision colour.
- Copy must be plain, specific, and honest.
- Pricing transparency is a trust artifact.
- Atlas should inform substance but not visual design.

But it is too permissive in ways that directly enabled V2's failure:

- It allows "immersive hero imagery" and "authentic environments" without enough category discipline.
- It says technology should rarely be shown, which is too strong. Technology must appear, but as evidence, context, and operational texture rather than cliche.
- It frames the website as dark-first and dark-only. Dark can be premium, but dark-only increases the risk of cinematic lifestyle mood, especially when paired with interior photography.
- It over-indexes on feeling before comprehension. TWNY needs feeling first, yes, but category comprehension must arrive almost immediately.

**Required correction:** the public site must be atmospheric and legible at the same time. The first viewport must reveal that TWNY is a technology practice. It cannot make the visitor wait until the footer or service pages to understand the category.

There is no `docs/governance/website-constitution.md` in this checkout. If a Constitution exists elsewhere, it should be amended with these non-negotiables:

- The first viewport must make TWNY legible as a technology practice.
- Pure interior, architecture, hospitality, or lifestyle imagery cannot carry the homepage alone.
- Technology must be present as operational evidence, not hidden completely.
- Atlas may inform the truth but must not dictate public visual language.
- Dark-only should not be a permanent rule. The site may be dark-first, but it needs tonal contrast and operational clarity.

## Positioning: Strong, But Needs Sharpening

The line "We run the cloud and the website so you can run the business" is directionally strong, but it can sound too narrow if repeated alone. TWNY also does AI enablement, automation, domains, backup, hosting, websites, ongoing advice, and Microsoft 365 operations.

The sharper positioning for art direction is:

> TWNY looks after the technology behind successful modern small businesses.

Support it with specifics:

> Microsoft 365, AI enablement, websites, hosting, domains, backup, automation, and ongoing advice, handled by one accountable operator.

This language does three jobs:

- It names the category quickly.
- It avoids MSP and agency language.
- It creates room for both modern-work operations and public web presence.

## Research Synthesis

### Kyle Skelly: Premium Through Reference, Ratios, And Pacing

Primary reference: [Kyle Skelly YouTube channel](https://youtube.com/@kyleslyf). Captures are saved in `art-direction-assets/`.

Kyle's work is useful because it treats premium web design as a set of observable decisions, not magic. The repeated ideas across the channel are:

- Start from references, but extract design logic rather than copying.
- Use proven product and web patterns as a quality floor.
- Critique layouts in terms of proportions, hierarchy, section pacing, and whether the hero is large enough for its job.
- Use cinematic imagery and motion to create desire, but keep the structure simple.
- Treat AI as a research and iteration partner. The designer still judges.
- Make the artifact visible. Show the page, the mockup, the UI, the asset, the motion.

The specific video added to the brief, ["Claude Design + Relume = Insane Website Workflow"](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=r3dQa6u146k), sharpens this direction. The important lesson is not the fantasy game-site example or the full-screen AI video asset. The lesson is the workflow:

- Generate the sitemap before the visual system.
- Review the wireframe before styling the page.
- Establish a design system before asking AI to produce multiple sections or pages.
- Use typography, spacing, colour, and component choices as constraints, not afterthoughts.
- Let AI accelerate variants, assets, and implementation, while the designer keeps editorial control.
- Hand the work into code for tightening: header behaviour, responsive polish, motion, asset compression, deployment, and domain connection.

For TWNY, this means Homepage V3 must not begin as a single beautiful hero moodboard. It needs a section sequence, wireframe logic, and design-system rules before imagery or animation are allowed to dominate. The site should feel premium because the system is coherent, not because one screen is impressive.

For TWNY, the useful extraction is not Kyle's energetic YouTube thumbnail style. It is the underlying discipline: premium work comes from proportion, pacing, reference quality, and visible craft.

TWNY should borrow:

- large confident hero hierarchy,
- clear visual proof of the thing being made,
- cinematic restraint where it helps,
- strong section pacing,
- fewer, better visuals,
- critique-led refinement.

TWNY should not borrow:

- hype language,
- "insane" or "genius" rhetoric,
- futuristic-for-its-own-sake imagery,
- AI-tool spectacle,
- novelty-driven motion.

### Jack Roberts: Premium Must Still Be A Business System

Primary reference: [Jack Roberts YouTube channel](https://youtube.com/@itssssss_jack). Captures are saved in `art-direction-assets/`.

Jack's work is louder than TWNY should ever be, but his thinking is valuable. He repeatedly treats a website as a business mechanism: it has to look premium, but it also has to be editable, deployable, sellable, and tied to a commercial outcome.

The useful ideas:

- A premium website is not just visual surface. It needs operational usefulness.
- Inspiration should be used to extract the "DNA" of typography, colour, spacing, emotional tone, and structure without copying.
- White space, type weight, balance, and motion are commercial signals, not decoration.
- The client must be able to understand, edit, and benefit from the result.
- The site should have a clear conversion mechanism, but not feel desperate.

For TWNY, this matters because V2 proved that a beautiful homepage can still fail commercially. The site must not become a mood film. It must make the offer visible, make the next step obvious, and show enough operational substance to justify trust.

TWNY should borrow:

- the idea that premium design must still convert,
- visible proof that the offer is real,
- structured inspiration extraction,
- clarity around the business mechanism,
- fast comprehension of value.

TWNY should not borrow:

- hype-forward conversion language,
- "changed forever" drama,
- algorithmic thumbnail energy,
- over-saturated AI/productivity claims,
- spectacle as proof.

### Creator Ecosystem And External Sites

The YouTube channels are not the whole reference. The external sites, products, and tools they point to reveal the production philosophy behind the videos.

Kyle's ecosystem includes [Relume's Claude Design export](https://www.relume.io/claude-design-export), [Future Designer](https://futuredesigner.io/), [Kyle's portfolio](https://www.kyleskelly.com/), [Mobbin](https://mobbin.com/), [Higgsfield](https://higgsfield.ai/), [ShowOff](https://www.showoff.video/), Framer, Figma, Claude Design, Claude Code, GitHub, and Cloudflare-style deployment thinking. The useful pattern is a designer-led pipeline: references, sitemap, wireframe, style guide, assets, code polish, compression, deployment, and handoff.

Jack's ecosystem includes [Glaido](https://glaido.com/), [Jack Roberts' newsletter archive](https://jackroberts.ai/), [AI Automation Vault](https://www.skool.com/ai-automation-vault/about), the [Claude Code curriculum site](https://claude-code-curriculum-deploy.vercel.app/), and recurring workflow tools such as Claude, GitHub, Vercel, MongoDB, Firecrawl, OpenRouter, Google AI Studio, ChatGPT, Zapier, Pinecone, and Antigravity. The useful pattern is productised operation: a clear promise, visible workflow, pricing, FAQ, conversion path, community proof, and a system that can actually run.

The extraction for TWNY:

- Premium modern work now comes from a complete production chain, not a static page comp.
- Inspiration libraries and AI tools should raise the quality floor, not decide the brand.
- Design systems prevent AI-generated sameness and prevent a page from becoming a one-off mood piece.
- Reusable components, deployment, CMS/content ownership, image/video compression, domain connection, and maintainability are part of the creative standard.
- A strong conversion path can exist without loud funnel language.

TWNY should not look like a creator course, AI-tool launch, or black-and-neon SaaS funnel. But the site should feel as disciplined as the best of that ecosystem: structured before styled, systematised before animated, and deployable before declared finished.

### Broader Web References: Useful As Warnings

The curation sites show what the current design world rewards, but they should not become TWNY's target.

- [Awwwards](https://www.awwwards.com/websites/sites_of_the_day/) is full of invention, but much of it optimises for novelty and spectacle.
- [Framer Gallery](https://www.framer.com/community/gallery/) demonstrates how quickly modern web design becomes a template marketplace: dark surfaces, bold cards, rounded UI, big thumbnails, and trendy interaction.
- [Godly](https://godly.website/) is a useful trend detector for high-polish motion and visual drama, but it leans toward "look at this" more than "trust this."
- [Land-book](https://land-book.com/) remains a useful curation source, though its capture was blocked by Cloudflare during research.
- [Siteinspire](https://www.siteinspire.com/) is the most useful meta-reference: quiet type, generous emptiness, clear categories, and confidence without spectacle.

The principle: learn quality control from these sources, not surface treatment.

### Non-Technology References

The best non-tech references are valuable when they reveal how premium businesses communicate substance.

- [The American Housing Corporation](https://www.americanhousing.com/) opens emotionally but still makes the operation tangible. It shows that a business can be mission-led and operationally concrete.
- [Tresmares Capital](https://www.tresmarescapital.com/) uses large type, quiet space, and clear offer language. It proves that premium can be bold and legible at once.
- [Old Tom Capital](https://www.oldtomcapital.com/) has strong atmosphere and confidence, but it is also a caution: category mood can overpower the practical offer.
- [BVN](https://www.bvn.com.au/) and [Site Architecture Studio](https://www.sitearchitecture.com.au/) prove why architecture photography works for architects. They are also warnings for TWNY: if the work is not architecture, architecture should not be the brand.
- [Brickworks](https://www.brickworks.com.au/) makes materials feel tangible and premium while keeping the product clear. TWNY needs the digital equivalent: make operational care feel tangible.
- [Vert Design](https://www.vertdesign.com.au/) shows products in context and evidence of making. TWNY needs that principle more than pure interior ambience.
- [Design + Industry](https://www.design-industry.com.au/) is a useful anti-pattern: black surfaces, uppercase confidence, and orange everywhere push toward macho engineering. TWNY should avoid that register.

## Desired Emotional Response

The visitor should feel three things, in this order:

1. **Handled.** This is the person who will quietly make the hidden technology behave.
2. **Current.** This practice understands modern work: Microsoft 365, AI, websites, domains, backup, automation, and the messy reality of small business.
3. **Accountable.** There is one operator, clear scope, plain pricing, and honest boundaries.

The feeling should be calm, not sleepy. Premium, not precious. Modern, not startup-loud. Human, not lifestyle-soft.

The visitor should not feel:

- "This is another IT support company."
- "This is an AI agency."
- "This is a web design studio."
- "This is a cafe/interiors/architecture brand."
- "This is an internal dashboard I am supposed to understand."

## The Visual Language

TWNY's visual language is made from four materials:

1. **Modern business environments.** Real or believable small-business contexts: studios, workshops, consulting rooms, site offices, professional desks, creative workspaces, builder/trade planning spaces, and compact teams at work.
2. **Operational evidence.** Cropped, restrained glimpses of the systems TWNY actually runs: Microsoft 365 surfaces, calendar, files, email identity, domain/DNS records, backup status, website preview, automation steps, AI prompt/result, security or recovery checks.
3. **Editorial composition.** Large type, strong rhythm, asymmetry, whitespace, and section pacing that feels authored rather than templated.
4. **Precise structure.** Hairlines, labels, tabs, quiet panels, tables, and service groupings used where clarity matters.

The magic is the combination. Photography alone becomes lifestyle. Structure alone becomes Atlas. The homepage must keep both in frame.

## Design System And Workflow

The Relume/Kyle reference changes the process standard: no finished visual direction should be approved before the system exists.

Required order:

1. Define the sitemap and section inventory.
2. Wireframe the homepage sequence and decide what each section must prove.
3. Establish type scale, spacing, colour behaviour, component rules, button states, navigation behaviour, and content patterns.
4. Select or generate imagery only after the section job is clear.
5. Add motion only after the still composition works.
6. Finish in code with responsive checks, compression, deployment, content ownership, domain readiness, and maintainability.

This is especially important for TWNY because the wrong visual system can pull the site into the two failed extremes very quickly: too much operational structure becomes Atlas; too much atmosphere becomes interiors. The design system is the guardrail that keeps the public website in the correct middle.

## Composition

Composition should feel like a premium editorial spread with operational precision underneath.

Use:

- one dominant idea per section,
- large confident type,
- generous negative space,
- asymmetrical balance,
- carefully cropped imagery,
- visible hierarchy between mood and proof,
- section pacing that alternates atmosphere and clarity.

Avoid:

- equal-card service grids as the primary composition,
- centered everything,
- dense dashboard panels,
- floating cards inside cards,
- collage for its own sake,
- too many simultaneous visual systems.

The site should feel designed, not assembled.

## Pacing

The page should breathe, but it must not drift.

Recommended rhythm:

1. **Immediate category signal.** The hero must say technology practice quickly.
2. **Atmosphere.** A modern-business scene gives emotional context.
3. **Concrete inventory.** Name what TWNY looks after.
4. **Operator proof.** Show the method and accountability.
5. **Service depth.** Explain Microsoft 365, AI, websites, domains, hosting, backup, automation, and advice as one managed operation.
6. **Trust and boundaries.** State what is included, what sits outside, and why.
7. **Pricing posture.** Make pricing transparency visible before the close.
8. **Invitation.** End with one calm next step.

Every atmospheric section needs a concrete follow-up. Every technical section needs enough space and warmth to avoid becoming a manual.

## Visual Hierarchy

The first visual hierarchy should be:

1. Who TWNY is for.
2. What category TWNY belongs to.
3. What TWNY handles.
4. Why it feels premium.
5. What to do next.

That means the first viewport cannot use an abstract headline like "Everything in its place" unless the surrounding copy and imagery immediately name the technology category.

Better headline territory:

- "The technology behind a modern small business, looked after."
- "Modern technology operations for small business."
- "Microsoft 365, AI, websites and the hidden systems behind your business, handled."
- "A quieter way to run the technology behind your business."

These are not final headlines. They define the correct altitude: human, specific, modern, and category-clear.

## Typography

Typography is the main premium signal. It should create confidence before decoration is needed.

Use:

- large display type with plain, declarative sentences,
- body copy that feels calm and specific,
- small labels for operational context,
- measured type contrast between editorial moments and precise information,
- comfortable line length and generous leading.

Avoid:

- all-caps section headers as personality,
- tiny elegant copy that hides the point,
- oversized abstract slogans,
- dense paragraph blocks,
- negative tracking or viewport-scaled font tricks,
- generic SaaS headline rhythm.

The display type should say something real. TWNY does not need taglines. It needs confident sentences.

## Photography

Photography is allowed, but it must be disciplined.

Photography should communicate:

- modern small-business competence,
- owner/operator reality,
- care, readiness, and order,
- the workday being supported,
- Australian small-business context where possible,
- warmth through real light and human scale.

Photography should not communicate:

- cafe lifestyle as the brand,
- architecture/interiors as the product,
- generic stock people at laptops,
- enterprise boardrooms,
- server rooms,
- abstract cloud infrastructure,
- luxury for luxury's sake.

The right photograph should make a visitor think:

> This is the kind of business TWNY helps keep running.

Not:

> This is a beautiful room.

### Good Image Subjects

Use subjects like:

- a founder reviewing work in a compact modern office,
- a builder or trade operator coordinating plans, quotes, and schedules,
- a professional-services desk with documents, calendar, and client work visible,
- a creative studio preparing a client presentation,
- a small team around one screen where the focus is the work, not the screen,
- hands working with a laptop, tablet, notebook, plans, tools, or product samples,
- a real website preview in context,
- a modern workspace with operational details present.

### Bad Image Subjects

Avoid:

- empty hospitality interiors,
- architectural hero spaces,
- staged coffee cups and notebooks,
- smiling headset support people,
- stock meetings with pointing at screens,
- server racks,
- glowing network nodes,
- AI robot/orb imagery,
- app-logo collages,
- generic abstract gradients.

## Should Technology Appear Directly?

Yes. The previous "technology should rarely be shown" rule is too blunt.

Technology should appear as **evidence of care**, not as decoration.

Show technology when it helps the visitor understand:

- TWNY runs Microsoft 365 as an operating layer.
- Websites, hosting, domains, and DNS are connected.
- Backup is about recovery.
- AI is implemented with readiness and context.
- Automation removes small repeated frictions.
- One operator understands how the pieces fit.

Do not show technology as:

- futuristic spectacle,
- stock dashboards,
- fake metrics,
- generic app-icon clouds,
- code screenshots nobody will read,
- "AI magic" imagery.

Technology should feel quiet, legible, and lived-in.

## How To Represent Each Service

### Microsoft 365

Represent Microsoft 365 as the daily work surface of a small business:

- email identity,
- calendar,
- shared files,
- Teams/chat presence,
- access and security settings,
- a clean tenant as the invisible foundation.

Avoid Microsoft app-logo wallpaper or licence-card imagery. Microsoft 365 is not the product on its own. The managed wrapper is the product.

### AI Enablement

Represent AI as a practical assistant inside real work:

- a prompt attached to a business task,
- an internal process being summarised,
- a document draft,
- a workflow recommendation,
- a readiness checklist,
- guarded language around data and permissions.

Avoid AI as fantasy: no robots, no glowing brains, no orbs, no "10x" claims, no dark-neon agent dashboards.

AI should feel useful and supervised, not magical.

### Websites

Represent websites as a public presence that stays alive:

- browser previews,
- mobile previews,
- content blocks in context,
- deployment/update evidence,
- uptime/care cues,
- a real business being represented well.

Do not make TWNY look like a marketing agency. The website is one part of the operation, not the whole identity.

### Hosting, Domains, And DNS

Represent these as ownership and continuity:

- domain renewal,
- DNS records,
- registrant ownership,
- SSL/certificate status,
- website health,
- handover/portability.

Keep it calm. This is trust material, not a technical showpiece.

### Backup

Represent backup as recovery readiness:

- restore points,
- protected files,
- recovery status,
- business continuity,
- the moment before something goes wrong.

Never sell storage. Sell recovery.

### Automation

Represent automation as a small business process becoming lighter:

- quote -> approval -> invoice,
- enquiry -> task -> follow-up,
- form -> document -> notification,
- manual repeat work becoming visible and orderly.

Avoid automation as "AI agents running everything." TWNY is practical.

### Ongoing Advice

Represent advice as a trusted operator's judgement:

- a short recommendation,
- a named owner,
- scoped next steps,
- "you need less than you think" honesty,
- boundaries written down.

This is one of the strongest trust signals. It should feel human, not consultancy-theatre.

## Colour

The current colour discipline remains mostly right, but V3 needs clearer behaviour.

### Ink

Ink carries authority. Use it for primary text and grounded moments. It should make TWNY feel serious without becoming corporate.

### Steel

Steel carries structure. Use it for supporting copy, dividers, hairlines, subdued labels, and operational surfaces.

### Mineral

Mineral is the technology current. It should carry movement, links, active states, service pathways, operational lines, and connective structure.

Mineral should dominate when the page is explaining how things connect:

- the operating loop,
- service groupings,
- navigation states,
- system cues,
- links,
- workflow details.

Mineral should not become a loud tech glow.

### Ember

Ember is still the decision colour.

Use Ember for:

- the primary CTA,
- a single active decision moment,
- rare commercial emphasis where action is required.

Do not use Ember for:

- decoration,
- icons,
- random highlights,
- broad warm mood,
- warning labels unless there is no better semantic option.

The V2 Ember-on-white contrast issue should be fixed before implementation. The decision colour must be accessible.

### Where Warmth Comes From

Warmth should come from:

- human-scale photography,
- natural light,
- timber, paper, work materials, and real small-business context,
- tone of voice,
- measured spacing,
- the operator's plain honesty.

Warmth should not come from making the UI orange, brown, beige, or cosy.

### Where Contrast Comes From

Contrast should come from:

- dark and light section shifts,
- type scale,
- photograph versus operational surface,
- calm whitespace versus precise detail,
- one Ember decision against restrained colour.

The site should not become a one-note dark-blue, beige, orange, or purple theme.

## Dark And Light

The site can remain dark-first, but it should not be governed by dark-only thinking.

Homepage V3 should use tonal shifts:

- a cinematic or deep opening where it helps create presence,
- lighter or quieter operational sections where clarity matters,
- Paper/Sand surfaces for pricing, scope, and service evidence,
- dark sections for mood, transition, and authority.

If the implementation phase insists on dark-only, the page still needs internal contrast: darker atmosphere, lighter panels, structured bands, and enough tonal relief that it does not feel like a long mood tunnel.

Dark should serve trust. It should not become the brand.

## Layering

Layering is how TWNY becomes both modern and concrete.

Use layers like:

- real photograph below,
- restrained scrim for legibility,
- large type above,
- small operational detail layer beside or within the composition,
- hairline pathways or labels that imply systems being looked after.

The operational layer can include:

- a calendar fragment,
- a domain status line,
- a backup recovery point,
- a website preview,
- a Microsoft 365 tenant note,
- an AI readiness/checklist prompt,
- a short scoped recommendation.

The layer must feel editorial and believable, not like a fake SaaS dashboard pasted over a photo.

## Whitespace

Whitespace is a trust signal. Use it generously, but not emptily.

Good whitespace:

- lets a strong idea land,
- separates atmosphere from proof,
- makes pricing and boundaries feel honest,
- gives the buyer time to understand.

Bad whitespace:

- hides the offer,
- creates long scrolls of mood,
- makes service information feel delayed,
- turns the site into a gallery.

The homepage should breathe with purpose.

## Navigation

Navigation must be visible enough to orient the visitor.

V2's almost-invisible navigation was too recessive. Premium navigation can be restrained without disappearing.

Use:

- wordmark-only identity,
- plain service labels,
- visible Pricing,
- visible Contact or "Tell us what you need",
- one primary CTA,
- a header that can sit over imagery but resolves quickly and legibly.

Recommended nav language for V3:

- Microsoft 365
- Websites
- AI
- Pricing
- Contact

If space is limited, group services but do not hide the category:

- Services
- Pricing
- Contact

Avoid:

- vague "Work" or "Studio" labels,
- agency-style navigation,
- hiding pricing,
- a header so transparent the visitor has no orientation.

## Interaction

Interaction should feel like quiet confirmation.

Use:

- subtle hover states,
- clear focus states,
- smooth but fast transitions,
- no layout shift,
- native disclosure where helpful,
- touch-friendly controls,
- accessible contrast.

Do not use interaction to entertain. Do not make visitors work to understand the offer.

The site should feel responsive, not playful.

## Animation Philosophy

Motion should feel like a system coming into alignment.

Use motion for:

- revealing relationships,
- gently bringing sections into view,
- showing before/after order,
- indicating progression through the operating loop,
- adding polish to transitions and states.

Avoid:

- bouncy motion,
- looping attention grabs,
- parallax for drama,
- slow reveals that hide content,
- animation as the only premium signal,
- AI/future-tech spectacle.

Kyle's work shows how cinematic motion can lift a simple composition. Jack's work shows how motion can make a site feel sellable and alive. TWNY should take the restraint from both: motion exists to clarify and refine, not to perform.

## Conversion Philosophy

TWNY should invite, not funnel.

The primary conversion is a low-pressure conversation:

> Tell us what you need. We'll recommend a starting point.

The conversion philosophy:

- one primary Ember CTA,
- secondary path to pricing,
- no fake urgency,
- no "free strategy call" posture,
- no pop-ups,
- no manipulative lead magnets,
- clear next step,
- explicit permission to be a bad fit.

Pricing should appear as trust, not as a discount anchor. The homepage should make the wrapper valuable before asking the visitor to compare numbers.

## Storytelling

The story is not "TWNY is clever with technology." The story is "your business works better when the hidden technology is looked after by someone accountable."

Tell that story through escalation:

- Start with the owner/operator's world.
- Reveal the hidden systems underneath modern work.
- Show that those systems are connected.
- Explain how TWNY takes responsibility for them.
- Make the outcome tangible: continuity, ownership, recovery, practical AI, a public website that stays alive, and calmer decisions.
- Close with a simple invitation.

The page should not tell a founder they are behind, careless, or technically naive. It should make them feel that the next responsible step is available and sane.

## Copy Philosophy

The copy should be plain, specific, and visually set with confidence.

Use:

- "We look after..."
- "You own..."
- "We run..."
- "This sits outside..."
- "If you need less, we'll say so."

Avoid:

- "solutions",
- "unlock",
- "leverage",
- "transformation",
- "seamless",
- "future-proof",
- "cutting-edge",
- "AI-powered everything",
- "business technology made easy",
- "take the hassle out".

The homepage should not explain Microsoft licensing. It should explain why a modern small business feels calmer when one capable operator looks after the technology behind it.

## What Visitors Must Understand

### After 3 Seconds

They should understand:

- TWNY is a technology practice.
- It is for small businesses.
- It feels premium, calm, and modern.
- It looks after the technology behind the business.

They should see or read at least one concrete category cue:

- Microsoft 365,
- AI,
- websites,
- domains,
- backup,
- technology operations.

If the first three seconds could belong to an architect, cafe, or interiors brand, the homepage fails.

### After 15 Seconds

They should understand:

- TWNY connects Microsoft 365, AI, websites, domains, hosting, backup, automation, and advice.
- It is one accountable operator, not a menu of disconnected services.
- It is not trying to sell them raw software licences.
- The page has a calm next step.

### After 60 Seconds

They should understand:

- what TWNY looks after,
- who it is for,
- what sits outside scope,
- why it costs more than commodity providers,
- that pricing is published,
- that ownership and portability matter,
- how to start a conversation.

## How TWNY Should Feel Different

### From Generic MSPs

Generic MSPs sell support, tickets, vendor badges, unlimited-sounding help, and vague "solutions." TWNY should feel smaller, more composed, more honest, and more modern. The site should make it clear that TWNY is not trying to absorb every technology problem. It runs a defined modern operating layer.

### From Enterprise Consultancies

Enterprise consultancies sell frameworks, transformation, and scale. TWNY should feel practical, owner-led, and close to the work. The buyer should feel they can speak to a person who will actually do the thing.

### From SaaS Dashboards

SaaS dashboards sell a product interface. TWNY sells an accountable operator. The public site can use operational surfaces, but it should never look like the visitor is being asked to learn a platform.

### From AI Startups

AI startups sell novelty, speed, and future shock. TWNY should sell caution, usefulness, readiness, and real implementation. AI is a service layer, not a religion.

### From Marketing Agencies

Agencies sell campaigns, brands, leads, and creative output. TWNY looks after operational technology and web presence. Websites are part of the offer, but TWNY should not look like it is primarily selling taste or campaigns.

## Creative Mistakes We Must Never Repeat

- **Atlas thinking leaking into the public site.** The public site is not an operating system. Do not lead with the loop, tenancy diagrams, tables, or internal logic.
- **Becoming an architecture magazine.** Architecture/interior imagery can be beautiful and completely wrong. If the image does not help explain modern business or the technology behind it, it does not belong.
- **Explaining before inspiring.** The site still needs atmosphere. A dry service catalogue will fail.
- **Inspiring without explaining.** V2 proved that mood without category is not enough.
- **Photography overpowering the message.** Images must support the offer, not become the offer.
- **Technology disappearing completely.** The site must be recognisably a technology practice within seconds.
- **Generic IT cliches.** No clouds, server racks, circuit boards, headset support, app-logo collages, cyber shields, or partner-logo walls as proof.
- **Generic lifestyle cliches.** No coffee-shop-as-brand, no empty interiors, no staged notebooks, no "quiet room" unless the technology context is visible.
- **Overusing cards.** Cards are useful for repeated items, but a page made of cards feels templated.
- **Underusing whitespace.** Crowding makes TWNY feel anxious and cheap.
- **Overusing whitespace.** Empty space without information makes TWNY feel vague.
- **Overusing Ember.** Ember is the decision colour, not the brand atmosphere.
- **Making AI the hero.** AI is important, but TWNY is not an AI startup.
- **Hiding pricing.** Transparent pricing is one of the strongest trust signals.
- **Hiding the operator.** A boutique practice needs accountability and human presence.
- **Letting motion carry quality.** The page must feel premium as a still composition.
- **Copying creators or galleries.** Extract principles, never layouts.
- **Skipping the system step.** Do not jump from reference mood to finished page. Sitemap, wireframe, design tokens, component behaviour, and content hierarchy must exist before visual polish.

## Homepage V3 Creative Direction

Homepage V3 should feel like stepping into the working layer of a successful modern small business: calm, current, and quietly technical.

The page should not open like a product dashboard. It should not open like a lifestyle magazine. It should open with a modern business moment and immediately reveal the technology being looked after.

### Opening Experience

The first viewport should combine:

- a modern small-business environment,
- visible operational technology evidence,
- a plain category-clear headline,
- one supporting line naming the service family,
- one Ember CTA,
- one secondary pricing or "how it works" path,
- navigation that confirms the category.

The hero image should not be an empty interior. It should show a business context where work is happening or about to happen. The technology layer can be subtle, but it must be present: a website preview, a Microsoft 365 work surface, a domain/backup/status cue, or a composed operational overlay.

The H1 should not be purely abstract. It can be elegant, but it must say what TWNY does.

Example direction:

> The technology behind a modern small business, looked after.

Supporting line direction:

> Microsoft 365, AI enablement, websites, domains, hosting, backup and automation, run by one accountable operator.

CTA direction:

> Tell us what you need

Secondary path:

> See pricing

These are directional examples, not final copy.

### Narrative

The homepage narrative is:

1. **You run the business.**
   TWNY looks after the technology behind it.

2. **Modern work has more hidden systems than most small businesses can comfortably manage.**
   Microsoft 365, identity, AI, website, domain, hosting, backup, automation, DNS, and everyday changes.

3. **TWNY makes those systems legible and accountable.**
   One operator, one source of truth, clear scope, honest boundaries.

4. **The value is calm continuity.**
   The day works, the website stays alive, the domain remains yours, recovery is possible, AI is used carefully, and advice is available when the business changes.

5. **The next step is a conversation, not a funnel.**
   Tell us where things stand. TWNY recommends a starting point.

### Visual Pacing

Before the homepage is styled, the sitemap and section sequence should be settled. Before motion is added, the design system should already define type scale, spacing, colour behaviour, component reuse, and responsive hierarchy.

The homepage should unfold in alternating registers:

- **Presence:** hero with modern business and operational layer.
- **Inventory:** the things TWNY looks after, named plainly.
- **Meaning:** why those things matter to the owner.
- **Method:** the operating loop, shown as a calm pathway rather than an Atlas diagram.
- **Proof of clarity:** boundaries, ownership, and pricing posture.
- **Invitation:** one calm close.

Do not make the visitor scroll through three mood sections before seeing services. Do not make the visitor read three technical sections before feeling anything.

### Imagery

Use three kinds of imagery:

1. **Hero business context.** A founder-led modern business environment with technology evidence visible.
2. **Operational close-ups.** Cropped details of work surfaces, devices, notes, plans, websites, files, domains, backup, or AI prompts.
3. **Human operator context.** A person responsible for work being handled, not a generic model acting cheerful.

Every image must answer one of these questions:

- What kind of business is this for?
- What technology is being looked after?
- What calm does TWNY protect?
- What operational proof is visible?

If an image only says "premium room," reject it.

### Hierarchy

Hierarchy should move from category to confidence:

1. TWNY is a technology practice.
2. TWNY serves small Australian businesses.
3. TWNY looks after Microsoft 365, AI, websites, domains, hosting, backup, automation, and advice.
4. TWNY is one accountable operator with clear scope.
5. Pricing is transparent.
6. The next step is easy.

The visual hierarchy should never make "beautiful photography" item 1.

### Copy Philosophy

Homepage copy should be short but concrete. It should name real services without becoming a list of licences.

Use the surface -> need pattern, but present it editorially rather than as equal cards:

- A managed mailbox: someone who already knows your setup when it breaks.
- A website: a public presence that stays up and stays yours.
- Backup: recovery for the day something is deleted or compromised.
- AI: useful assistance once the data and permissions are ready.
- Domains: ownership and renewal handled properly.
- Automation: repeated work made lighter.

Keep language grounded in the business owner's day.

### Design Principles For V3

- The page must be system-first: sitemap, wireframe, style rules, component reuse, then imagery and motion.
- The first viewport must be category-clear.
- The hero must include technology evidence.
- Atmosphere must be followed by proof.
- Services must be grouped as one operation, not separate commodity cards.
- The operating loop belongs after the promise, not as the opening identity.
- Pricing must appear as trust, not as a discount tactic.
- The page should use photography, but never let photography become the product.
- The page should use operational surfaces, but never become a dashboard.
- Motion should clarify relationships and make the page feel alive.
- Images, video, CMS/content ownership, deployment, domains, compression, and maintainability are part of the creative standard, not cleanup tasks.
- Ember should appear only where a decision is requested.
- The close should invite a conversation, not push a funnel.

### Homepage V3 Implementation Guardrails

- Do not copy reference screenshots.
- Do not reuse V2 composition.
- Do not open with abstract lifestyle copy.
- Do not lead with Atlas concepts such as operating loop, managed tenancy, or service architecture.
- The first viewport must answer: "What kind of business is this?"
- The first viewport must make TWNY legible as a technology practice.
- The hero should combine business context with visible operational technology evidence.
- Pricing should be visible as a trust signal, but not as a discount tactic.
- Atmosphere must always be followed by proof.
