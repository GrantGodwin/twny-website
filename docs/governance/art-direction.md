# TWNY Website Art Direction

**Status:** Governing visual authority for the TWNY public website.
**Audience:** Designers, frontend developers, writers, and AI assistants working on the public website.

This document defines how the public website *looks and feels.* The broad creative
standard — who we're for, what we sell, the personality, the copy line — lives in
[`creative-direction.md`](./creative-direction.md); this is its visual execution. Where
the two conflict on a **visual** question, this document wins. On a **what's-true /
what-we-offer** question, defer to the Creative Direction and to Atlas. Neither document
specifies HTML, components, or layouts — they define the standard precisely enough to
build without guessing.

## Creative North Star

The whole brand reduces to one idea: **TWNY exists to make the technology behind a business
feel simple.** Technology is the medium, simplicity is the outcome, competence is how we
get there. Every visual decision below serves it — a visitor should leave thinking
*"technology feels simpler,"* not *"they sell Microsoft 365."* The brand philosophy
(*technology is the medium, simplicity is the outcome, competence is how*) and the
canonical brand statement — *Simplifying the technology behind your business.* — are
defined in [`creative-direction.md`](./creative-direction.md#brand-language).

## The Decision

TWNY should look like **the calm operating layer that makes a modern small business's
technology feel simple** — and unmistakably like a technology practice while doing it.

Not an internal operating system. Not an architecture magazine. Not an MSP template. Not
an AI startup. Not a marketing agency.

The direction is **Modern Operator Editorial**:

- **Modern** — the work is Microsoft 365, AI, websites, domains, backup, automation: the
  systems that let a business run properly now.
- **Operator** — TWNY quietly looks after the hidden technology, owns the details, and
  stays accountable after launch.
- **Editorial** — composed, premium, selective, human; strong pacing and hierarchy build
  confidence before the visitor reads every detail.

If a visitor admires the site but cannot tell what TWNY does, the art direction has failed.
If they understand the services but feel nothing, it has also failed. Both must be true at
once.

## The Place Between the Extremes

Two earlier directions failed in opposite ways, and the gap between them is exactly where
TWNY lives.

- **Atlas leaked in.** Treating the website like a public version of the internal system —
  structural, diagram-led, precise — made TWNY feel like software, not a practice. Keep
  Atlas's *truth*, never its *surface.*
- **It became an interiors magazine.** Atmosphere, restraint, and photography drifted so
  far from technology that the hero could belong to a cafe or an architect. Keep the calm,
  scale, and confidence; lose the lifestyle ambiguity.

The resolution: **premium through composition, pacing, type, and restraint; legible through
visible operational evidence, service language, and modern-work context.** Atmosphere and
category arrive together, not in sequence.

## Non-Negotiables

These are fixed. Everything else is judgement.

1. The **first viewport** makes TWNY legible as a technology practice — at least one
   concrete category cue (Microsoft 365, AI, websites, domains, backup, automation) on
   screen within seconds.
2. **Atmosphere is always followed by proof** — no long scroll of mood before substance,
   no wall of substance before feeling.
3. **Technology appears as quiet evidence of care** — never hidden completely, never
   spectacle, never a fake dashboard.
4. **Atlas informs the truth, never the surface** — no operating-loop diagrams, tenancy
   vocabulary, or internal structure as the public identity.
5. **Typography leads; photography supports** — the image never becomes the story.
6. **Ember is the only decision colour,** and accessible wherever it appears.
7. **One continuous Ink-first environment** — no white or Paper sections (see *Theme &
   Surfaces*).
8. **No fabricated proof,** and **no reuse of reference assets** as production material.

## The Visual Language

TWNY's visual language is made from four materials, and the magic is the combination —
photography alone becomes lifestyle, structure alone becomes Atlas:

1. **Modern business environments** — believable small-business contexts: studios,
   workshops, consulting rooms, site offices, professional desks, compact teams at work.
2. **Operational evidence** — cropped, restrained glimpses of the systems TWNY runs: a
   Microsoft 365 surface, a domain/DNS line, a backup recovery point, a website preview, an
   automation step, an AI prompt.
3. **Editorial composition** — large type, strong rhythm, asymmetry, whitespace, authored
   pacing. One dominant idea per section; designed, not assembled.
4. **Precise structure** — hairlines, labels, quiet panels used where clarity genuinely
   earns them, never as default decoration.

## Theme & Surfaces

The site is **one continuous Ink-first environment. This is locked.**

- **No white sections. No Paper sections.** The page never switches to a light theme for
  "relief."
- Separation and pacing come from **alternating Ink with lifted Ink/Steel surfaces** — a
  panel a few steps lighter or darker than its neighbour — not from changing the theme.
- The effect should feel like moving through rooms in the same building, not flipping
  between two different sites.
- Contrast comes from **tonal shifts within the Ink family, type scale,
  photograph-versus-surface, and whitespace-versus-precise-detail** — never from a white
  page.
- Hold **WCAG 2.1 AA throughout,** including text over photography (scrims exist for this).
  There is no public theme toggle.

This supersedes any earlier "dark-first but use Paper/Sand for relief" guidance. Relief is
a *lifted Ink surface,* not a light one.

## Colour

Roles are fixed; warmth and contrast come from tone and light, not from new colours.

- **Ink — authority.** Primary grounding and text. Serious, never corporate.
- **Lifted Ink — separation.** Section and panel separation within the one environment.
- **Steel — structure.** Supporting copy, labels, hairlines, metadata, quiet operational
  surfaces.
- **Mineral — the technology current.** Movement, emphasis, links, hover and active states,
  connective lines — the way the operating layer reads as *connected.* Mineral leads when
  the page shows how things join up, but never becomes a loud tech glow.
- **Ember — decisions only.** The primary CTA and the rare moment an action is genuinely
  requested. Never decoration, icons, highlights, or mood. Must be accessible against its
  surface (the V2 Ember-on-white problem cannot recur).

**Warmth** comes from human-scale photography, natural light, real materials, voice, and
spacing — never an orange, brown, or beige UI. **Never** let the site become a one-note
theme of any single hue.

## Typography

**Typography is the primary design element. Photography supports typography; typography
never supports photography.** This ordering settles any close call.

- Large editorial display type carrying plain, declarative sentences — a real idea set
  large, never a tagline.
- Restrained, specific body copy; comfortable line length and generous leading.
- Small Steel labels for operational context.
- Strong hierarchy and generous whitespace do the heavy lifting before any decoration.

Avoid: all-caps headers as personality, tiny precious copy that hides the point, oversized
abstract slogans, dense paragraph blocks, negative tracking or viewport-scaled font tricks,
generic SaaS headline rhythm.

## Photography

We are **not** after technology photography, architecture photography, lifestyle
photography, or stock business people. We are after one thing: **beautifully photographed
businesses at work.**

- **The business is always the subject** — real or believable Australian small businesses
  doing the work, in natural light, with lived-in detail.
- **Technology exists naturally inside the scene, as quiet evidence** — a laptop mid-task,
  a website on a screen, job sheets, a booking on a phone. Never staged, never the point.
- **The image supports the story; it never becomes the story.** If a photo only says
  "beautiful room," reject it. If it could appear on a competitor's site, reject it.
- **Authored, not stock** — intentional light, a clear focal point, honest moments, a calm
  restrained grade of a piece with the Ink palette.
- **Truth over polish** — no fabricated clients, no staged offices passed off as ours.

The test: *"This is the kind of business TWNY helps keep running,"* not *"This is a
beautiful room."*

**Good subjects:** a founder reviewing work in a compact office; a trade operator
coordinating quotes and schedules; a professional-services desk with real client work; a
creative studio prepping a presentation; a small team focused on the work, not the screen;
hands on a laptop, plans, or product samples.

**Avoid:** empty hospitality or architectural interiors; staged coffee-and-notebook still
lifes; headset support people; stock "pointing at a screen" meetings; server racks; glowing
nodes; AI orbs or robots; app-logo collages; abstract gradients.

## Technology as Quiet Evidence

Technology must be visible — but as **evidence of care,** not decoration or spectacle. Show
it to make one thing legible: these connected systems are being looked after by someone who
understands how they fit. Represent each service by its *outcome,* cropped and calm:

- **Microsoft 365** — the daily work surface (email identity, calendar, shared files, a
  clean tenant). The managed wrapper is the product, not the app logos.
- **AI** — a practical, supervised assistant inside real work (a prompt on a real task, a
  draft, a readiness check). Never fantasy, never "10x."
- **Websites** — a public presence that stays alive (a real preview in context, uptime and
  care cues). Not an agency showreel.
- **Hosting, domains, DNS** — ownership and continuity (renewal, registrant ownership,
  certificate status, portability). Trust material, kept calm.
- **Backup** — recovery readiness (restore points, the moment before something goes wrong).
  Sell recovery, never storage.
- **Automation** — a repeated process made lighter (enquiry → task → follow-up). Practical,
  not "agents running everything."
- **Advice** — an operator's judgement (a short scoped recommendation, "you need less than
  you think"). One of the strongest trust signals; human, not consultancy-theatre.

Never: futuristic spectacle, stock dashboards, fake metrics, app-icon clouds, code walls,
cyber shields, partner-logo walls.

## The Signature Component

TWNY is still missing the one element that would make the site unmistakably its own — a
recognisable visual device that becomes shorthand for the brand, the way a distinctive
masthead or a signature spread does for a great publication. Defining it is the **first
creative task of implementation.**

**Its role.** It should express, in a single calm composition, **the connected operating
layer behind a business** — the idea that the mailbox, the website, the domain, the backup,
and the everyday work are separate things held together and looked after as one. It is the
visual embodiment of the brand philosophy — *simplicity as the outcome*: many moving parts,
made to feel like one quiet whole.

**What it must not be.** A fake dashboard. A grid of service cards. A row of chips. An
Atlas-style diagram. Those are the generic defaults and the exact failure modes this
element exists to replace.

**What it must feel like.** Premium, editorial, calm, and unmistakably TWNY — composed and
authored, not assembled; something that could only belong to this brand; and that holds up
as a *still image* with no motion.

**Process rule.** This component must be **discovered before pages are built.** Design and
agree it first, then let it set the visual logic for the homepage and service pages — it is
the keystone, not a decoration bolted on afterwards. We deliberately do not specify its form
here.

## Homepage Execution

The *purpose* of each homepage movement is owned by the Creative Direction's
[Homepage philosophy](./creative-direction.md#13-homepage-philosophy): **create confidence
→ reveal the hidden technology layer → show how TWNY simplifies it → build trust through
accountability → invite a conversation.** This section governs how that sequence *looks and
paces.*

- **Alternate registers, one environment.** Move between atmosphere (a business-at-work
  scene) and proof (named systems, the signature component, boundaries), each on its own
  lifted-Ink surface, so the page has cadence without ever leaving the Ink world.
- **Open with presence and category together.** Near-full-height hero: a business-at-work
  image, transparent navigation resolving on scroll, one confident headline, one supporting
  line that names the service family, one Ember invitation, and at least one quiet
  operational cue.
- **Every atmospheric section has a concrete follow-up; every technical section has space
  and warmth** so it never reads as a manual.
- **The signature component carries the "connected layer" moment** — not a card grid or
  chip list.
- **Pricing appears as trust, late** — after the value of the managed operation has landed,
  never as a discount anchor.
- **Close as an invitation, not a funnel** — one Ember CTA, conversational, surrounded by
  space.

The page must pass three comprehension checkpoints:

- **~3 seconds:** technology practice, for small business, premium and calm, looking after
  the technology behind the business — with at least one concrete cue on screen. If the
  first seconds could belong to an architect, cafe, or interiors brand, it fails.
- **~15 seconds:** TWNY connects Microsoft 365, AI, websites, domains, hosting, backup,
  automation and advice as one accountable operation — not a menu — and there is a calm next
  step.
- **~60 seconds:** what's looked after, who it's for, what sits outside scope, why it costs
  more than a commodity provider, that pricing is published, that ownership and portability
  matter, and how to start a conversation.

## How TWNY Should Feel Different

- **From generic MSPs:** smaller, more composed, more honest, more modern. TWNY runs a
  *defined* modern operating layer — not unlimited-sounding support and vendor badges.
- **From enterprise consultancies:** practical, owner-led, close to the work. You can speak
  to the person who will actually do it.
- **From SaaS dashboards:** TWNY sells an accountable operator, not a product interface. Use
  operational surfaces; never ask the visitor to learn a platform.
- **From AI startups:** caution, usefulness, readiness, real implementation. AI is a service
  layer, not a religion.
- **From marketing agencies:** TWNY looks after operational technology and web presence.
  Websites are part of the operation, not a campaign showreel.

## Motion & Interaction

Motion should feel like a system quietly coming into alignment — and the page must be fully
premium as a *still* composition with motion off.

- Use motion to reveal relationships, bring sections gently into view, and polish state
  transitions — easing that feels expensive.
- Interaction is quiet confirmation: subtle hover and clear focus states, fast transitions,
  no layout shift, touch-friendly, accessible.
- Avoid bounce, looping attention-grabs, parallax drama, slow reveals that hide content, and
  any future-tech spectacle. Never let motion carry quality.

## Navigation

Visible enough to orient, restrained enough to stay premium — never the near-invisible
header that left V2 disorienting.

- Wordmark-only identity (`twny`), plain service labels, **visible Pricing,** a visible
  Contact / "Tell us what you need," and one Ember CTA.
- The header may sit over the hero, then resolve quickly to a legible solid state.
- Preferred labels: **Microsoft 365 · Websites · AI · Pricing · Contact.** If space is
  tight, group as **Services · Pricing · Contact** — but never hide the category, and never
  use vague "Work/Studio" agency labels.

## Workflow: System Before Style

No visual direction is approved before the system exists. In order:

1. Settle the **sitemap** and section inventory.
2. **Wireframe** the sequence — decide what each section must *prove.*
3. **Discover the signature component,** and establish type scale, spacing, colour
   behaviour, surface levels, component rules, and navigation behaviour.
4. Select or make **imagery** only once each section's job is clear.
5. Add **motion** only after the still composition works.
6. Finish in **code:** responsive checks, compression, content ownership, domain readiness,
   maintainability.

The right system is the guardrail that keeps the site out of both failure modes; the wrong
one slides into Atlas or into interiors within a single screen.

## Creative Mistakes We Must Never Repeat

- **Atlas surface in public** — leading with the loop, tenancy diagrams, tables, or internal
  logic.
- **Becoming an architecture or interiors magazine** — beautiful imagery that doesn't help
  explain the business or the technology behind it.
- **Explaining before inspiring** — a dry service catalogue.
- **Inspiring without explaining** — mood with no category (the V2 failure).
- **Photography overpowering the message** — the image becoming the offer.
- **Technology disappearing** — not recognisably a technology practice within seconds.
- **Generic IT cliches** — clouds, server racks, circuit boards, headset support, app-logo
  collages, cyber shields, partner-logo walls.
- **Generic lifestyle cliches** — coffee-shop-as-brand, empty interiors, staged notebooks.
- **A page made of cards or chips** — useful for repeated items, templated as a default.
- **Mis-using whitespace** — crowding reads anxious and cheap; empty mood reads vague.
- **Overusing Ember** — it is the decision, not the atmosphere.
- **Making AI the hero** — important, but TWNY is not an AI startup.
- **Hiding pricing, or hiding the operator** — both are trust signals.
- **Letting motion carry quality** — the still must already be premium.
- **Copying creators or galleries** — extract principles, never layouts.
- **Skipping the system step** — no jump from reference mood to finished page.

## Influences & Asset References

Reference captures live in [`art-direction-assets/`](./art-direction-assets/) and are **for
analysis only — never copy their layouts, imagery, colour, motion, or content, and never use
them as production assets.**

The durable lessons drawn from them are already baked into this document: *system before
style; extract principles, never surface; premium must still convert; architecture
photography belongs to architects; quiet category clarity beats spectacle.* The point-in-time
analysis behind those lessons (creator workflows, gallery surveys, non-tech comparators) is
**research, not governance** — treat the asset folder as evidence, not instruction, and let
it age without rewriting these principles to match it.

## Final Standard

The homepage succeeds when a small-business owner looks at the first screen and thinks:

> *This is a modern technology practice. They understand businesses like mine. They'll make
> the hidden systems feel handled — and somehow simpler.*

It fails if the first thought is "nice cafe," "nice architecture," "nice dashboard," "nice
AI demo," "nice agency site," or "what do they actually do?"

TWNY should feel unmistakably like TWNY: a boutique Australian technology practice with
taste, restraint, operational competence, human accountability, and enough modern edge that
the buyer feels they are not falling behind — and a quiet sense that, in TWNY's hands,
technology gets simpler.
